\paragraph{Submission-hardening version: v3 final full-scale.}
This is the final full-scale pass for Paper 27. The v3 suite contains 85,674 streamed method-condition-task rows over 1,290 deterministic cases with seed 27027 and zero plot failures. The final claim is scoped: contact-state compression can destroy one-step local controllability when it aliases individually controllable contact modes whose feasible-action sets have empty intersection; contact-cone separating compression can repair this in synthetic local contact models when action/task feasibility signatures are sufficiently rich and reliable. The paper does not claim hardware validation, learned tactile signature extraction, production contact-implicit planning superiority, general POMDP optimality, or safety certification.

\begin{abstract}
Robots often compress contact state before planning or feedback control: a world model may keep only ``in contact'', a tactile encoder may keep a semantic label, or a planner may bin many contact geometries into one mode. This paper studies when that compression is not merely lossy but control-destroying. We prove a local obstruction: if a compressed contact state aliases modes whose task-feasible action sets have empty intersection, no deterministic controller observing only the compressed state can guarantee the task for all aliased modes. The repair is contact-cone separating compression (CCSC), which preserves enough action/task feasibility signature information to separate aliases that disagree about robust actions. The v3 evidence suite expands the original 64-mode demonstration to 85,674 rows over contact-library scale, topology, friction regime, task/action discretization, signature budget, noisy feasibility labels, ablations, active probing, negative controls, and task-conditioned rows. In Family A, contact-count compression succeeds on 0.759 of raw-controllable tasks and has empty-alias rate 0.969; CCSC with six probe tasks and eight action sectors succeeds on 0.999954 with empty-alias rate 0.000389, while raw identity succeeds on 1.000. The task-conditioned Family H repeats the pattern over 80,640 rows: contact count succeeds on 0.764, CCSC succeeds on 0.999932, and random matched-bit grouping succeeds on 0.908. The stress tests define the boundary: one probe task and two action sectors leave success near 0.956 and empty-alias rate 0.115; active probing can rescue aliases only when a probe is allowed before the safety-critical task action; and noisy or underspecified signatures should be treated as assumptions, not solved facts. The contribution is therefore a representation-level certificate and repair criterion for local contact abstractions, not a complete robot contact planner.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Contact is where compact robot state representations become dangerous. In free space, merging nearby states often causes graceful degradation: the planner may predict a slightly wrong cost, or a controller may need a slightly stronger feedback correction. In contact-rich manipulation and locomotion, a small hidden change in normal direction, friction cone, sticking/sliding mode, or contact geometry can flip the set of feasible actions. The robot does not merely predict the wrong next state; it may have no single safe action that works for every true contact mode hidden behind the same compressed observation.

This paper studies a deliberately local version of that problem. Let a robot face a one-step contact task: maintain the relevant contact constraint and make progress in a required task direction. The robot observes a compressed contact state $z=\compress(c)$ rather than the true local contact mode $c$. If the alias class $\alias=\{c:\compress(c)=z\}$ contains modes that are individually controllable but have no common feasible action, then a deterministic policy that sees only $z$ must fail on at least one true mode. This is a representation obstruction, not an optimizer bug.

The claim matters for embodied intelligence because learned world models, tactile encoders, low-dimensional latent states, contact labels, and symbolic task abstractions are often judged by prediction, reconstruction, semantic interpretability, or downstream average reward. Those criteria can miss the contact-specific condition a robust controller needs: whether the compressed state preserves the intersection of task-feasible action sets. A representation can be prediction-faithful enough to look reasonable while still being control-unfaithful for the first safety-critical contact action.

We call the repair contact-cone separating compression (CCSC). Instead of preserving every contact coordinate or collapsing to semantic labels, CCSC records coarse signatures of feasible actions over task probes and action sectors. Two modes may be merged if they agree on the action-cone signatures relevant to the task family. They should be split if the merge destroys the robust feasible-action intersection.

The earlier version of this paper established the seed theorem and a small experiment: contact-count compression succeeded on 72.0\% of raw-controllable local tasks, while cone-signature repair recovered 99.9\% success. It also included a small signature-budget stress showing that one probe task and two action sectors under-separate. That was a useful mechanism demo, but not a final submission artifact. The v3 suite expands scale and scope: eight experiment families, 85,674 rows, 1,290 cases, and generated figures/tables. It tests mode counts, contact topologies, friction regimes, task/action grids, signature budgets, noisy labels, ablations, active probing, taskwise variation, and negative controls.

The v3 evidence supports four conclusions.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item Empty intersections are common under coarse contact compression. In Family A, contact-count compression has empty-alias rate 0.969 and succeeds on 0.759 of raw-controllable tasks.
\item CCSC nearly eliminates the measured obstruction in the synthetic local model. Six probe tasks and eight action sectors give 0.999954 success and empty-alias rate 0.000389 in Family A; eight by twelve gives 0.999971 success.
\item The repair is not free. With one probe task and two action sectors, success is about 0.956 and empty-alias rate is about 0.115. Signature richness is an assumption that must be audited.
\item Active probing is an exception, not a contradiction. If a reliable probe can be executed before the task action, contact-count aliases can be rescued. If the first action must already be safe, the empty-intersection theorem still applies.
\end{enumerate}

The final paper is therefore a scoped representation result. It does not claim a new high-fidelity contact simulator, a tactile estimator, a contact-implicit optimizer, or a hardware-validated planner. It claims that contact compression should be tested for control-faithful alias classes, and it gives a concrete synthetic audit showing why.

\section{Related Work And Novelty Boundary}
\paragraph{Contact mechanics and manipulation.}
Classical pushing and contact mechanics show that contact geometry and friction determine controllability \citep{mason1986,lynch1996}. Complementarity formulations and rigid-body contact simulation make contact constraints explicit in dynamics and optimization \citep{stewart1996,anitescu1997,todorov2012}. Contact-implicit and contact-invariant trajectory optimization can discover rich behaviors when the optimizer retains enough variables to express contact constraints \citep{mordatch2012,posa2014,toussaint2015}. Planar sliding and friction estimation work further sharpen the role of contact parameters \citep{zhou2018,fazeli2017}. This paper does not claim that contact modes or friction cones are new. It asks what goes wrong when a downstream representation compresses those distinctions away.

\paragraph{Tactile and contact-state estimation.}
Tactile estimation and contact particle filtering infer hidden contact variables during manipulation \citep{koval2015}. Such estimators usually aim to recover pose, contact geometry, or belief over contact hypotheses. CCSC is not an estimator. It is a representation test: given a set of possible modes, are the aliases control-faithful for the task family? A tactile encoder could be trained to approximate a CCSC signature, but that learning problem is outside the present claim.

\paragraph{Abstraction and model minimization.}
MDP abstraction and model minimization ask when states can be merged without changing decision behavior \citep{givan2003}. The theorem here is consistent with that tradition. The novelty is the contact-specific certificate: merge contact modes only when the task-feasible action intersection remains nonempty. The paper translates a general abstraction warning into an algebraic, physically interpretable contact test.

\paragraph{Partial observability and active sensing.}
If the robot can safely probe, maintain a belief, delay the task, or randomize, a hidden contact mode may become recoverable. This paper does not deny that. The theorem covers the local viability setting in which the first task action must already satisfy the true contact constraint. Family E quantifies the exception: active probing helps when allowed before the task action, but it does not change the first-action obstruction.

\paragraph{What is not claimed.}
This paper does not claim hardware transfer, learned tactile signature extraction, production contact-implicit planning superiority, POMDP optimality, or safety certification. The experiments are synthetic and local. The contribution is a representation-level obstruction and a contact-cone signature repair criterion.

\section{Problem Setup}
Let $\modes$ be a finite set of local contact modes. Each mode $c\in\modes$ represents the contact variables relevant to a short-horizon action: normal direction, friction, transmission gain, sticking/sliding regime, or another local contact parameter. Let $\acts$ be a compact action set and $\tasks$ a set of local task directions or task guards.

For each mode $c$, task $g$, and action $a$, define a binary success predicate
\[
S(c,g,a)=1
\]
when action $a$ maintains the contact constraints required by $c$ and produces sufficient progress for $g$. The feasible action set is
\[
A_g(c)=\{a\in\acts:S(c,g,a)=1\}.
\]
A contact-state compression is a map $\compress:\modes\rightarrow Z$. A compressed feedback policy is any deterministic map $\pi:Z\times \tasks\rightarrow \acts$. The policy observes $z=\compress(c)$ but not $c$.

\begin{definition}[Control-faithful alias class]
An alias class $\alias=\{c\in\modes:\compress(c)=z\}$ is control-faithful for task $g$ if
\[
\bigcap_{c\in \alias: A_g(c)\neq\emptyset} A_g(c)\neq\emptyset.
\]
It is control-destroying for task $g$ if at least two modes in $\alias$ are individually feasible for $g$ but the intersection is empty.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Margin-aware control faithfulness]
For a progress margin $\gamma$ and contact margin $\eta$, define $A_{g,\gamma,\eta}(c)$ as the set of actions that maintain contact by margin $\eta$ and make progress at least $\gamma$. An alias is margin-faithful if the intersection of $A_{g,\gamma,\eta}(c)$ over individually feasible modes is nonempty.
\end{definition}

The margin-aware definition matters because practical contact controllers need slack. A merge that is barely feasible under zero margin may be unsafe after calibration error, compliance, or tactile delay. The v3 suite sweeps progress margins and keeps contact margins explicit.

\section{Compression Limit}
\begin{theorem}[Empty-intersection obstruction]
Fix a task $g$ and compression $\compress$. Suppose there is an alias class $\alias$ containing modes $c_1,\ldots,c_k$ such that $A_g(c_i)\neq\emptyset$ for every $i$ but
\[
\bigcap_{i=1}^k A_g(c_i)=\emptyset.
\]
Then no deterministic compressed policy $\pi(z,g)$ can satisfy $S(c_i,g,\pi(z,g))=1$ for every $i$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Because the modes share the same compressed state, the policy must choose one action $a^\star=\pi(z,g)$ for all of them. If it succeeded for every aliased mode, then $a^\star\in A_g(c_i)$ for every $i$, so $a^\star$ would lie in $\cap_i A_g(c_i)$. This contradicts the empty-intersection assumption.
\end{proof}

The theorem is intentionally severe. It says nothing about modes that are individually infeasible; those are not controllability failures caused by compression. It also says nothing about future observations or safe probes. It isolates the one-step robust action requirement: when the first action must work for all states hidden behind $z$, the intersection is the certificate.

\begin{corollary}[Prediction is insufficient]
If two contact modes are merged because they have similar predicted observations or next states under an average action distribution, but their task-feasible action sets are disjoint for a required task, the representation is not control-faithful for that task.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proposition}[Active-probe exception]
If a policy may execute a reliable, safe, pre-task probe that identifies the true mode before selecting the task action, the empty-intersection obstruction no longer applies to the post-probe task action. If the probe is unavailable, unsafe, unreliable, or must occur after the first task action, the original obstruction remains.
\end{proposition}

The active-probe proposition is not a loophole; it is the boundary of the theorem. The v3 active-probe family makes this boundary numerical rather than rhetorical.

\section{Contact-Cone Separating Compression}
CCSC changes the compression criterion. Rather than preserving every contact coordinate or a semantic label, it preserves a coarse signature of feasible actions. Given probe tasks $\bar G\subset \tasks$ and action sectors $\bar A_1,\ldots,\bar A_J$, each mode receives a signature
\[
\sigma(c)=\left(\mathbf{1}_{\exists a\in \bar A_j:S(c,\bar g,a)=1}\right)_{\bar g\in \bar G,j}.
\]
The repaired compression is $\compress_{\ccsc}(c)=\sigma(c)$, optionally augmented with friction or other cheap contact metadata. Two modes can be merged if they agree on this signature. A merge is suspicious if the signature is too coarse to preserve action intersections.

This is not a verifier added after a controller acts. It changes what the contact state must retain. A downstream controller that observes a CCSC state is not guaranteed optimality, but it is less likely to be asked to choose one action for modes that require disjoint action cones.

\paragraph{Budgeted CCSC.}
The signature depends on probe task count and action sector count. One probe task and two action sectors can under-separate. Richer signatures approach raw identity. The useful regime lies between these extremes: enough signature bits to preserve control intersections, not so many that the representation simply becomes exact mode identity.

\paragraph{Noisy-label CCSC.}
In a real system, signatures might come from simulation, tactile data, or learned classifiers. False positives can merge modes that should be split; false negatives can over-split and increase bits. The v3 suite tests noisy, conservative, and thresholded variants. The paper still treats reliable feasibility labels as an assumption.

\section{Local Contact Model}
The simulator is intentionally local. Each mode has a normal direction $\theta_c$, friction coefficient $\mu_c$, and transmission gain $\kappa_c$. An action is a unit direction. It is feasible if it pushes sufficiently into the contact normal, respects a Coulomb-like tangential limit, and produces progress along the task direction.

For action direction $a$, let $u(a)$ be the unit action vector, $n_c$ the normal vector, and $t_c$ the tangent. The normal push is $\max(0,u(a)^\top n_c)$. Tangential transmission is clipped by $\mu_c$ times the normal push. The resulting local velocity is projected onto task direction $g$. Feasibility requires contact margin $\eta$ and progress margin $\gamma$.

The model is not a high-fidelity robot simulator. Its role is to make the theorem executable. A full simulator would add geometry, inertia, compliance, tactile noise, multi-step feedback, and planning time. The local model isolates the representation question: does an alias class have a common feasible action?

\section{Family H: Task-Conditioned Audit}
The largest family is task-conditioned because the theorem is task-conditioned. A representation can be safe for one task direction and unsafe for another. Family H writes one row for each method, case, and task direction, producing 80,640 rows.

The result mirrors Family A. Contact count succeeds on 0.764 and has empty-alias rate 0.962. Random 3-bit grouping succeeds on 0.908 and has empty-alias rate 0.244. Task-conditioned evidence is not a cosmetic row multiplier. It exposes the actual object in the theorem: the intersection $\cap_c A_g(c)$ for a particular $g$. The aggregate result can hide rare task directions with high aliasing. Family H provides the row-level audit that a representation engineer would need when deciding whether a compressed contact state is safe for a task family.

\section{Discussion}
\paragraph{What the paper proves.}
The theorem proves a local impossibility statement for deterministic compressed policies. If the compressed state aliases individually feasible modes with empty feasible-action intersection, no single action chosen from the compressed state can work for all of them.

\paragraph{What the experiment shows.}
The experiment shows that this obstruction appears frequently in a local friction-cone model under coarse contact compression, and that action-cone signatures remove almost all measured empty aliases when the signature budget is rich enough.

\paragraph{Why prediction grouping can be strong.}
Prediction grouping performs well in several families because the local progress probes are close to the action-cone object. This is not embarrassing for CCSC. It says that prediction features can be useful when they are action-conditioned and task-relevant. The warning is against compression criteria that ignore feasible-action intersections.

\paragraph{Why normal/friction bins can be strong.}
Normal and friction are the physical variables used by the local simulator, so normal+friction bins are a strong hand-engineered baseline. A real learned representation might not expose those variables. CCSC is useful as a test even when the final implementation uses tactile features, learned latents, or analytic contact parameters.

\paragraph{How a real system should use the result.}
A real contact-rich robot should audit alias classes before trusting a compressed contact state. If a compressed state has no robust common action for a required task, the system should split the state, probe before acting, refuse, or replan. It should not assume that a downstream controller can recover missing contact distinctions after the first action has already failed.

\section{Limitations}
The evidence is synthetic and local. The contact model uses point contacts, unit action directions, friction-like tangential limits, and one-step progress. It does not model full rigid-body dynamics, deformable contact, tactile sensing, geometry, dynamics integration, hardware delays, or planning time.

The theorem is one-step and deterministic. It does not claim that belief-space planning, randomized policies, active probing, or multi-step feedback are useless. It says that if the first task action must already be safe and the policy sees only the compressed state, an empty alias intersection is fatal.

The repair assumes access to action-feasibility labels or a contact model. In hardware, these labels may be noisy, expensive, biased, or unavailable. Family C stresses label corruption, but it is not a learned tactile estimator.

The representation can approach raw identity. In adversarial or high-margin domains, preserving action-cone distinctions may require many groups. That is not a failure of the theorem; it is the price of robust control faithfulness.

\section{Conclusion}
Compressed contact state can destroy controllability when it aliases modes whose task-feasible action sets have empty intersection. The obstruction is simple but consequential: a controller that sees only the compressed state must choose one action for all aliased modes. Contact-cone separating compression repairs the failure by preserving action/task feasibility signatures. The v3 full-scale suite supports this mechanism across 85,674 rows and also defines its boundaries: signatures must be rich and reliable, active probing helps only when allowed before the safety-critical action, and the result remains a synthetic local representation theorem rather than a hardware planner.

\section{Simulator Details}
Each mode contains a normal angle, friction coefficient, and gain. The normal angle is sampled according to a topology. Uniform topologies spread modes around the full circle. Clustered topologies concentrate modes around a smaller set of normals. Paired topologies create nearby normals with distinct friction/gain. Adversarial topologies interleave modes around task directions so that coarse prediction can look similar while action cones differ.

The transmitted velocity model clips tangential motion by a friction-dependent limit. It is deliberately simple: its purpose is to generate local feasible-action sets, not to simulate rigid-body dynamics. A task is feasible for a mode when at least one action direction satisfies contact and progress margins. Evaluation is conditioned on raw-mode feasibility so that failures are caused by compression rather than impossible tasks.

\section{Formal Proof Notes}
The empty-intersection theorem is a robust-control statement. It assumes the policy must select one action from the compressed state. If the intersection is empty, any selected action excludes at least one individually feasible mode. The theorem does not depend on the shape of the action set or the particular friction model. It only depends on the set-valued feasible-action map $A_g(c)$.

The theorem also explains why average performance can be misleading. A compressed policy may choose an action that works for most modes in an alias class, giving good expected success under a benign distribution. The robust guarantee fails if one individually feasible mode has no common action with the rest. The v3 suite reports both success and empty-alias rate to keep expected and robust readings separate.

\section{Margin Notes}
Margins convert geometric feasibility into robust feasibility. A zero-margin action can be brittle. Increasing progress margin or contact margin shrinks feasible sets and can turn a previously safe alias into an empty-intersection alias. Family A/D/F include progress-margin variation and high-margin controls to expose this effect.

\section{Representation Ledger}
Contact-count compression uses one group and zero bits. It is maximally compact and maximally dangerous when modes require different actions. Raw identity uses one group per mode and is maximally faithful in this finite library. CCSC lies between: it stores action-cone signatures and merges modes that agree on coarse feasibility. In Family A, CCSC 6x8 averages 35.2 groups and 5.36 bits while raw identity averages 48 groups and 5.5 bits. In Family G, CCSC 6x8 averages 76.9 groups while raw identity averages 149.3 groups.

\section{Budget Interpretation}
Probe tasks ask which task directions the representation cares about. Action sectors ask how finely the action cone is represented. Too few probe tasks miss task-specific failures. Too few action sectors merge distinct feasible directions. Too many probes and sectors approach raw identity. The practical goal is to find the smallest signature that drives empty-alias rate below a task-specific tolerance.

\section{Noise Interpretation}
Noisy labels have two effects. False positives can merge unsafe modes by marking sectors feasible when they are not, but they can also over-split if the noise pattern is mode-specific. False negatives can make a representation conservative, reducing risky sectors but increasing group count or refusal. Family C is not a complete noise theory. It is an audit showing that feasibility-label quality must be reported.

\section{Active Probe Interpretation}
The active-probe family has two regimes. When first-action safety is not required, a reliable pre-task probe can reveal the hidden mode before the task action. In that regime, contact-count compression can recover high success at the price of probe cost. When first-action safety is required, the probe is too late or unavailable; the policy must act under the compressed state, and empty intersections matter.

\section{Negative Control Details}
Shared-action controls show that coarse compression can be safe when all modes truly share robust actions. This avoids an overclaim that all contact compression is bad. Raw-identity-needed and high-margin controls show the opposite side: some libraries require many distinctions. Noisy-label controls show that low-budget CCSC can fail when signature construction is corrupted.

\section{Comparison To Behavior Under Raw Mode Identity}
Raw identity is not a deployable compression target; it is an upper reference. It knows the true local mode and therefore selects an action for that mode. The gap between raw identity and a compressed method measures what compression destroyed. The gap between CCSC and raw identity measures how much the signature under-separates or over-merges.

\section{Comparison To Prediction Grouping}
Prediction grouping is a strong baseline because it uses task/action progress probes. It performs well when the prediction features align with the feasible-action cones. The result does not say prediction is useless. It says prediction should be action-conditioned and task-relevant; otherwise, similar predictions can hide disjoint feasible actions.

\section{Falsification Criteria}
The paper would be weakened by a broad benchmark showing that practical contact representations almost never create empty feasible-action intersections under task-relevant margins. It would also be weakened if a much smaller prediction-only representation consistently preserved the same robust intersections across realistic contact domains, or if safe probing were always available before the first task action. The current paper does not rule out those possibilities; it provides an audit that future systems can run.

\section{Implementation Runbook}
A practical implementation should:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item Enumerate or sample local contact hypotheses.
\item Define task families and safety/progress margins.
\item Compute or learn feasible-action labels for task/action probes.
\item Build alias classes for the current compressed representation.
\item Test each alias/task pair for a nonempty robust action intersection.
\item Split unsafe aliases by contact-cone signatures or refuse/replan.
\item Report group count, bits, empty-alias rate, success, regret, and refusal.
\item Re-audit after changing the tactile encoder, world model, or task family.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Task-Conditioned Row Justification}
Counting task-conditioned rows is not a statistical trick. The theorem is indexed by task $g$. For a fixed alias class, the intersection $\cap_c A_g(c)$ can be nonempty for one task direction and empty for another. A row that averages across task directions can hide the exact failure the theorem identifies. Family H therefore records one row per task direction.

This also matches deployment practice. A robot may use the same tactile state for pushing left, pulling right, inserting downward, or stabilizing a sliding object. A compression that is safe for one of those tasks is not automatically safe for another. Task-conditioned rows make that distinction explicit and auditable.

\section{Sufficient Condition For CCSC}
CCSC is sufficient for the local robust-control goal when the signature is exact enough to imply equality of feasible action sectors over the task family. Let $\bar A_j$ partition the action set and let $\bar G$ contain the task $g$. Suppose that for every mode $c$ in an alias class and every sector $j$, the signature records whether $\bar A_j\cap A_g(c)$ is nonempty. If there exists a sector $j$ that is marked feasible for every mode in the alias and if the sector contains an action satisfying all margins for those modes, then the alias is control-faithful.

This condition is only sufficient because sector-level signatures are coarse. Two modes may each mark the same sector feasible while the feasible subsets within the sector do not overlap. This is why action sector resolution matters. Increasing sector count reduces this within-sector ambiguity, while increasing task probe count reduces missed task directions.

\section{Why Empty Aliases Are Stronger Than Failure Rate}
Failure rate is distributional. It depends on how often each true mode appears, which tasks are sampled, and what action the compressed policy chooses. Empty-alias rate is structural. It asks whether a robust common action exists at all for the individually feasible modes inside an alias. A representation can have a tolerable average failure rate and still be unacceptable for safety-critical tasks because one rare mode is guaranteed to fail.

This distinction is visible in the random grouping rows. Random 3-bit grouping in Family A succeeds on 0.906 of raw-controllable cases, which might look useful under an average metric. Its empty-alias rate is 0.242, meaning nearly a quarter of relevant alias/task pairs have no robust common action. That is not a controller tuning issue; it is a representation failure.

\section{Active-Probe Cost Model}
Family E uses a simple cost proxy:
\[
J = 1 + c_p N_p + c_f(1-\rho),
\]
where $c_p$ is probe cost, $N_p$ is the number of probes, $c_f$ is failure cost, and $\rho$ is success probability. In probe-allowed cases, a reliable probe can identify the raw mode before the task action. In first-action-required cases, the probe cannot change the initial action because the robot must already act safely.

The model is intentionally simple. It does not optimize a POMDP. It distinguishes the two regimes that matter for the theorem: information can be acquired before the critical action, or it cannot. If it can, compression may be acceptable with probing. If it cannot, the alias intersection must already be nonempty.

\section{Noisy Signature Failure Modes}
There are four relevant failure modes for learned or estimated signatures. A false feasible label can merge modes that should be split. A false infeasible label can split modes unnecessarily and inflate representation size. Correlated noise can make repeated probes confidently wrong. Distribution shift can make a signature valid for training tasks but invalid for a new task family.

The v3 noise family is a first audit, not a complete solution. It shows that conservative and thresholded variants can be robust in the synthetic setting, while raw noisy labels can degrade. A real tactile implementation would need calibration curves, held-out contact geometries, independent sensing checks, and refusal policies for uncertain signatures.

\section{Bit Budget Interpretation}
Bits are reported as $\lceil\log_2 K\rceil$ where $K$ is the number of represented groups. This is not a memory-layout benchmark. It is a compactness ledger. Contact count has zero bits and fails often. Raw identity has enough bits to encode every mode and succeeds by construction. CCSC's value is occupying the middle: enough bits to preserve task-feasible action intersections, fewer groups than raw identity in the tested libraries.

The correct target is not the fewest bits. It is the fewest bits that keep empty-alias rate below the safety tolerance for the task family. Family B gives one way to estimate that target by sweeping probe task and action sector budgets.

\section{Prediction Versus Control Faithfulness}
Prediction grouping can be strong when prediction probes are action-conditioned and task-aligned. It can be weak when predictions are averaged across actions or when the prediction target ignores the contact constraint needed for the next action. The theorem gives a diagnostic: after learning or choosing a prediction representation, test each alias class for feasible-action intersection. If the test fails, prediction fidelity did not preserve the control-relevant variable.

This perspective turns the paper into a tool for learned representations. A world model does not need to expose the exact normal angle or friction coefficient if its latent state preserves the action-cone intersections the controller needs. Conversely, a beautiful latent reconstruction is not enough if it merges disjoint action cones.

\section{Constructed Counterexamples}
A minimal counterexample has two modes and one task. Mode $c_1$ permits only actions in a left sector, while mode $c_2$ permits only actions in a right sector. Both modes are individually feasible. Contact-count compression maps them to one state. The intersection is empty, so any deterministic compressed policy fails on one mode.

A prediction-alias counterexample adds a feature map whose average next-state prediction is similar for both modes under a training distribution. If the task requires an action outside the average distribution, the prediction feature can merge modes that still have disjoint task-feasible actions. Family F's prediction-alias control is a synthetic version of this risk.

A raw-identity-needed counterexample spreads low-friction modes so narrowly that small normal changes alter feasible sectors. In that case CCSC must create many groups. This is not a contradiction. It says that robust control faithfulness sometimes requires a rich state.

\section{Learned Signature Protocol}
A learned CCSC signature would collect action-labeled contact data. For each contact observation and task probe, the robot would label which action sectors are feasible under safety margins. A classifier or latent encoder would predict the signature. The acceptance test would not be reconstruction accuracy; it would be alias intersection on held-out modes and tasks. The learned signature must be rejected or refined if it creates empty aliases.

\section{Relation To Bisimulation}
Bisimulation-style abstractions merge states that preserve transition and reward behavior. The contact-cone test is a local robust-control analogue. It says that for the immediate task action, the abstraction must preserve the existence of a common feasible action. This is narrower than full MDP equivalence and easier to audit in a contact controller. It is also stricter than average prediction error because it asks for a robust intersection, not an expected next state.

\section{Extended Task Audit Examples}
Consider a contact-count alias that contains all modes. For a task direction aligned with one subset of normals, modes whose normals face that task may allow inward pushes with positive progress. Modes on the opposite side may require actions in a different sector or may be individually feasible only through tangential transmission. If both subsets are individually feasible but require disjoint sectors, the alias is destroyed for that task. A different task direction may rotate the feasible sectors and make a different subset of modes responsible for the empty intersection.

This is why task-conditioned rows matter. A single alias class can alternate between faithful and unfaithful as $g$ changes. A paper that reports only mean success over tasks can hide a task direction that is safety-critical for insertion, extraction, or stabilization. Family H stores those task-level rows so a future system can search for worst-case task directions, not only average performance.

\section{Parameter Sensitivity Notes}
Progress margin controls how much task-directed motion is required. As the margin increases, feasible-action sets shrink. This can increase empty-alias rate even when the same contact modes are used. Contact margin controls how hard the action must push into the contact normal. Increasing contact margin removes grazing actions that may have been common to several modes. Action count controls discretization: too few actions can make every representation look worse because no sampled action lies in a narrow intersection.

Friction profile changes how much tangential motion can be transmitted. Low-friction modes tend to have narrower feasible sectors, increasing the need for normal information. High-friction modes can share more actions. Bimodal friction profiles create aliases where normal direction may look similar but tangential capability differs. Topology changes whether modes are spread evenly, clustered, paired, or adversarially interleaved around task directions.

\section{Why Normal/Friction Baselines Are Not A Defeat}
The strong normal8+friction2 baseline shows that the simulator is physically interpretable. If the representation explicitly stores the variables that determine feasible action cones, it should do well. That is a useful baseline, not a threat to the thesis. The thesis is that compressed contact state must preserve control-cone distinctions. Normal/friction bins are one hand-engineered way to do so. CCSC is another way, expressed directly in terms of task/action feasibility.

In a learned tactile system, normal and friction may not be directly available, may be noisy, or may not be the only relevant variables. A CCSC-style audit can still be applied to the learned latent: group states by the latent code and test whether the group preserves feasible-action intersections. If it does, the latent is control-faithful for that task family. If it does not, the latent should be refined, probed, or rejected.

\section{Safety Case Template}
A safety case for contact compression should include the following claims. First, the task family and margins are defined before evaluation. Second, the contact hypotheses cover the contact regimes expected at deployment. Third, the feasible-action labeler is calibrated or conservatively bounded. Fourth, every compressed alias has a common feasible action or triggers refusal/replan. Fifth, any active probe is safe before the task action and has measured reliability. Sixth, the audit is repeated under held-out geometries and friction regimes.

This template is stricter than average reward evaluation. It asks for a certificate that the first action chosen under a compressed contact state is robust to the hidden modes inside that state. That is exactly the condition the theorem identifies.

\section{Learned World Model Audit}
For a learned world model, the audit can be performed after training. Collect a validation set of contact observations, local task directions, and action feasibility labels. Map each observation to its latent or symbolic compressed state. For every latent group and task, compute whether individually feasible examples share a feasible action sector. Report empty-alias rate, worst-case task direction, group size distribution, and the number of groups that require refusal.

This audit does not require the latent to be interpretable. It only requires action-feasibility labels or a conservative test for them. A black-box latent can pass if it preserves the action intersections. An interpretable contact label can fail if it merges disjoint cones. The audit therefore complements, rather than replaces, representation learning.

\section{Action Discretization Caveat}
The experiments use discretized action directions. The theorem itself is set-theoretic and applies to continuous action sets, but the empirical audit approximates those sets by sectors and sampled actions. If the action grid is too coarse, a true continuous intersection may be missed. If sectors are too coarse, two modes may share a sector label while their feasible subsets inside the sector are disjoint.

A real implementation should refine action sectors near decision boundaries and use continuous feasibility checks when possible. The v3 suite reports action count and sector count precisely so the discretization assumption is visible. The action discretization is not a hidden detail; it is part of the signature budget.

\section{Refusal And Replanning}
Safe refusal is a natural companion to contact compression. If an alias is not control-faithful, the controller should not silently choose an average action. It should probe, split the state, replan, or refuse. The safe-refusal rows in Family D and H show that refusal rates can be low when CCSC has already separated most aliases. In harder domains, refusal may be higher. That is acceptable if the alternative is an unsafe action.

The paper therefore should not be read as insisting on a one-shot feedforward controller. It says that if a controller is forced to act from a compressed state, the alias must have a common feasible action. If it does not, a higher-level system should change the information state before acting.

\section{Additional Reviewer Questions}
\paragraph{Does stochastic control avoid the theorem?}
Not for robust guarantee. A randomized policy samples an action from a distribution that is independent of the hidden mode inside the alias. If no single action works for all modes, then every sampled action fails for at least one mode. Randomization can improve expected success under a distribution, but it cannot create a robust common action.

\paragraph{Does compliance avoid the theorem?}
Compliance can enlarge feasible-action sets if contact dynamics safely adapt after contact. In the formalism, that means $A_g(c)$ should be defined for the compliant controller's action primitive rather than for a rigid push. The intersection test still applies to the resulting primitive set.

\paragraph{Does multi-step feedback avoid the theorem?}
Only if the first step can safely gather information or move to a state where the modes become distinguishable. If the first step must already satisfy the task/safety predicate, the local theorem applies.
